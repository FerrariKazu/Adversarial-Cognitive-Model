What The Architecture Implies
RHAN achieves robustness through four combined mechanisms. Each one maps to something real in biological vision:
The convolutional stem → V1 and V2 in the visual cortex
Your stem's local smoothing, which handles Regime 1 (ε < 0.03), mirrors what V1 does biologically. V1 neurons are essentially oriented edge detectors with local receptive fields — they act as spatial frequency filters. The reason humans don't notice imperceptible adversarial perturbations is likely that V1's spatial frequency tuning naturally filters out high-frequency adversarial noise before it even reaches higher processing areas. Your architecture built this in deliberately, and it worked exactly as predicted.
The transformer attention → V4 and IT cortex
The global attention that handles Regime 2 (ε ≥ 0.05) corresponds to what the higher ventral stream areas do — integrating information across the entire visual field to extract shape and object identity independent of local texture. V4 is specifically shape-selective. IT (inferotemporal cortex) responds to whole objects regardless of position, size, or viewpoint. Your transformer is doing a computational approximation of this. The fact that it helps at high epsilon confirms that global integration genuinely contributes to robustness — not just architecturally but as a principle.
The recurrent feedback → The feedback connections from IT back to V1
This is the most scientifically important part. Your recurrent feedback loop, where higher-level global representations modulate lower-level spatial features, directly mimics the massive feedback projections from IT cortex back to V1 in biological vision. The brain sends roughly as many signals downward (IT→V4→V2→V1) as upward. The function of this feedback is exactly what your model uses it for — using high-level predictions to clean up and disambiguate noisy low-level signals. When you look at a blurry or partially occluded object, your brain uses its prediction of what the object should look like to fill in missing information. This is why humans don't collapse at ε=0.05 the way feedforward models do.
The semantic head → Language and conceptual grounding
Even without full CLIP grounding, your cosine similarity classifier with learnable prototypes loosely approximates how human object categories are grounded in conceptual rather than purely visual representations. Human categories are defined by function, context, and language — not just visual appearance. A cat is a cat because of what it does and means, not just because of its fur texture. This is why humans maintain performance even when visual quality degrades severely — we classify by concept, not by pixel statistics.

What Human Vision Teaches Us — The Deeper Lessons
Your entire project, from the psychophysical data to RHAN, converges on a set of principles that biology figured out through 500 million years of evolution. Here's what the data is actually telling you:
Lesson 1: Robustness requires hierarchy, not just depth
Your models show that adding more layers of the same type (EfficientNet's compound scaling) doesn't help robustness — it actually hurts it. What helps is having qualitatively different processing stages that handle different aspects of the visual signal. The human visual cortex has a strict hierarchy: V1 handles orientation and spatial frequency, V2 handles contours and boundaries, V4 handles color and shape, IT handles object identity. Each stage is specialized. Your RHAN's stem→attention→feedback structure is a crude approximation of this, and the robustness improvement confirms the principle.
Lesson 2: Feedback is not optional — it's the mechanism
The d′ gap between humans (>0.30) and your best model (≈0.05) is the quantitative measure of what feedback processing provides. You've essentially measured the robustness value of recurrent processing in normalized units. The 10× gap means feedback provides roughly one order of magnitude more robustness than the best feedforward architecture. This is a specific, measurable claim that CORnet-S (when you integrate it) will either confirm or challenge.
Lesson 3: The brain doesn't optimize for clean accuracy
Every model that was optimized for clean CIFAR-10 accuracy — EfficientNet (96.81%), ViT (97.80%) — collapsed fastest under attack. The human brain doesn't optimize for maximum recognition rate in ideal conditions. It optimizes for robustness across all conditions, including noise, occlusion, low light, and adversarial perturbation. This is why humans sacrifice some absolute accuracy (your 73% baseline) for generalizability. Your RHAN-adv made the same trade: 88% clean vs 39% at ε=0.05 instead of 97% clean vs 8.56%. The brain made this architectural choice billions of years ago.
Lesson 4: Confidence and accuracy must be coupled
Your overconfidence finding — models being 99% confident while 0% accurate — points to something fundamental. Human metacognition is a feature, not a luxury. When a human says "I'm not sure," that uncertainty signal propagates through the decision system and triggers different behavior (look more carefully, seek more information, defer judgment). AI models have no such mechanism. The confidence-accuracy dissociation is not just a quirk of softmax — it reflects the absence of a proper uncertainty estimation system. Building uncertainty-aware neural networks that know when they don't know is one of the most important open problems in AI safety, and your data quantifies exactly how large this gap is.
Lesson 5: Adversarial examples reveal what models actually learned
Your confusion matrix findings — DOG→CAT, AUTOMOBILE→TRUCK, TRUCK→SHIP — tell you what features each model actually uses for classification. The fact that adversarial attacks push models toward semantically similar classes means the attack is corrupting discriminative features while preserving coarser category-level features. This is informative: it means models do learn some genuine semantic structure, but their fine-grained discriminative features are texture-based and fragile. Humans' fine-grained discrimination is shape-based and robust. The confusion patterns are a window into the representational geometry.

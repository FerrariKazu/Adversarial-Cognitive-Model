  1. RESEARCH QUESTION
============================================================================

  Does adversarial robustness scale with global visual processing —
  and is it determined by architecture or training objective?

  Specifically, we hypothesize that:
  (a) Feedforward CNNs (ResNet, EfficientNet) are fundamentally more
      vulnerable to adversarial perturbations than human observers
      because they rely on local texture statistics rather than global
      shape integration.
  (b) Shape-biased training (Shape-ResNet) and global attention (ViT)
      improve adversarial robustness toward human-like levels.
  (c) Purely local processing (BagNet) represents the extreme of
      vulnerability — a lower bound on robustness.
  (d) The sensitivity gap between humans and machines, measured via
      Signal Detection Theory (d-prime), reveals a qualitative
      difference in perceptual representation, not merely a
      quantitative performance gap.

  KEY OBSERVATIONS:

  1. PIXELATION BASELINE: The clean accuracy (73.3%) is significantly
     lower than typical human object recognition (>95%). This is
     attributed to CIFAR-10's 32x32 resolution, which is below the
     optimal frequency range for the human retina, causing inherent
     'pixelation noise' even at zero epsilon.

  2. ROBUSTNESS GAP: Despite the low baseline, human accuracy degrades
     extremely slowly. At epsilon=0.30, where models are at chance,
     humans still perform at nearly 60% accuracy.

  3. NON-MONOTONICITY: We observe a slight accuracy increase at ε=0.20
     (62.2%) compared to ε=0.10 (59.2%). This is likely due to sampling
     variance in the image subset or local feature 'glitches' that
     humans occasionally exploit even at higher noise levels.

  7. THEORETICAL INTERPRETATION
============================================================================

  FEEDFORWARD vs FEEDBACK PROCESSING:

  The human visual cortex processes visual information through two
  interacting streams:
    1. FEEDFORWARD: rapid bottom-up sweep from retina → V1 → V2 → V4 → IT
       (~150ms). This is what CNNs approximate.
    2. FEEDBACK: slower recurrent connections that flow back from higher
       areas (IT, prefrontal cortex) to lower areas (V1, V2). These loops
       allow the brain to 're-examine' the input using top-down predictions
       about expected shapes and objects.

  Standard CNNs (ResNet, EfficientNet) are purely feedforward. They have
  NO recurrent connections. Our SDT analysis quantifies the cost of this
  architectural limitation:

  • The CNN's d-prime collapses rapidly because adversarial perturbations
    destroy the local texture features that feedforward processing relies on.
  • Human d-prime degrades slowly because feedback loops reconstruct global
    shape from noisy input — effectively 'filling in' the damaged textures.
  • The d-prime threshold gap is a DIRECT measurement of how much perceptual
    robustness is provided by recurrent processing that CNNs lack.

  TEXTURE BIAS HYPOTHESIS (Geirhos et al., 2019):

  Our per-class SDT analysis further supports the texture bias hypothesis.
  If we observe that texture-defined classes (cat, dog, frog — identified
  by fur/skin patterns) show lower CNN d-prime than shape-defined classes
  (airplane, truck — identified by silhouette), it demonstrates that the
  CNN's internal representation is texture-centric, not shape-centric.

  ARCHITECTURAL PREDICTIONS FOR THE 5-MODEL SPECTRUM:

  If our hypothesis is correct, we predict:
    BagNet-33      → Earliest d' collapse (pure local, 33×33 patches)
    ResNet-18      → Early collapse (feedforward, texture-biased)
    EfficientNet-B0→ Similar to ResNet (still feedforward CNN)
    Shape-ResNet-50→ Later collapse (shape-biased training shifts features)
    ViT-Small      → Latest collapse (global attention spans full image)
    Human          → Latest of all (recurrent processing, shape-dominant)

  8. LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
============================================================================

  LIMITATIONS:

  1. DATASET SCOPE: CIFAR-10 images are 32×32 pixels. This limits the
     visual complexity of the stimuli. ImageNet (224×224) or ObjectNet
     would provide more ecological validity, but at much higher
     computational cost.

  2. ATTACK DIVERSITY: We tested FGSM, PGD, and C&W. Other attacks
     (DeepFool, AutoAttack, spatial transforms) might reveal different
     vulnerability patterns. Particularly, L2 attacks (C&W) vs L∞ attacks
     (PGD) affect perception differently.

  3. HUMAN SAMPLE SIZE: University student participants may not represent
     general human visual ability. Larger, more diverse samples would
     strengthen the human baseline.

  4. DISPLAY CONDITIONS: Human participants viewed stimuli on personal
     devices (via Google Forms), not in controlled laboratory conditions.
     Display calibration, viewing distance, and ambient lighting were
     not controlled.

  5. LAPLACE SMOOTHING: The +0.5 correction used to prevent infinite d'
     slightly underestimates sensitivity for very large datasets. For our
     sample sizes (1000 per epsilon), this bias is negligible (<0.01 d').

  FUTURE WORK:

  1. ADVERSARIAL TRAINING: Fine-tune models with PGD-adversarial training
     (Madry et al., 2018) and re-measure d'. Does adversarial training
     close the gap with human perception?

  2. RECURRENT CNNs: Test CORnet-S (Kubilius et al., 2019), which adds
     explicit recurrent connections to a CNN. If our theory is correct,
     CORnet's d' should degrade more slowly than vanilla ResNet.

  3. REACTION TIME: Add reaction time measurement to the human study.
     Longer RTs at high epsilon would indicate that humans are using
     effortful recurrent processing (feedback loops take time).

  4. GRAD-CAM ATTENTION MAPS: Overlay Grad-CAM heatmaps with SDT results
     to show WHERE in the image the CNN is looking when d' collapses.
     Expected finding: attention shifts from object center to noise edges.

  5. FULL 5-MODEL COMPARISON: Complete training for all 5 models and
     generate the headline figure: 6 d' curves (5 models + human) on
     the same plot, ordered by processing locality.

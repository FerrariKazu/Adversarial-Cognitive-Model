\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
Deep neural networks achieve superhuman performance on clean visual datasets
yet exhibit extreme vulnerability to adversarial perturbations.
This study quantifies the divergence between biological and machine vision
by evaluating seven neural architectures against human observers
($n{=}18$, 1\,800 psychophysical trials) under identical adversarial
constraints applied via 100-step Projected Gradient Descent (PGD).
Applying Signal Detection Theory (SDT), we measure perceptual sensitivity
($d'$) and sensitivity-collapse thresholds across a locality-to-globality
processing spectrum spanning BagNet-33, ResNet-18, EfficientNet-B0,
Shape-ResNet-50, CORnet-S, ViT-Small, and our proposed
Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network (RHAN).
All standard architectures collapse below the reliable-discrimination
threshold ($d'{<}1.0$) before $\varepsilon{=}0.031$,
while human sensitivity remains stable ($d'{=}2.019$) at $\varepsilon{=}0.30$.
EfficientNet-B0 is the most fragile model despite the highest clean accuracy
($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0062$);
ResNet-18 is paradoxically the most robust standard architecture
($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0295$).
Shape-biased training via Stylized-ImageNet
(Shape-ResNet-50, $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0082$)
fails to improve over the standard texture-biased baseline,
constituting a negative result that challenges the sufficiency of
texture-bias as an explanation for adversarial fragility.
Our proposed RHAN-adv, trained with a three-stage curriculum adversarial
procedure, achieves $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0764$ and retains
$52.0\%$ accuracy at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ where all feedforward models
are near zero~---~demonstrating that biological-like recurrent feedback,
rather than feedforward scaling or shape-biased training, is the primary
mechanism for closing the human-machine robustness gap.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

Deep convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have achieved remarkable performance
on standard image classification benchmarks, frequently matching or exceeding
human accuracy on clean stimuli~\citep{he2016deep,tan2019efficientnet}.
However, this apparent capability is fragile: minute, human-imperceptible
perturbations crafted through gradient-based optimisation~---~known as
adversarial examples~---~can cause state-of-the-art models to fail
catastrophically with near-absolute
confidence~\citep{goodfellow2015explaining,madry2018towards}.
This mismatch exposes a fundamental qualitative divergence between artificial
and biological visual systems.

In biological vision, object recognition is not a purely feedforward process.
The initial feedforward sweep through the ventral pathway
(V1$\!\to$V2$\!\to$V4$\!\to$IT) is immediately followed by massive top-down
recurrent feedback connections~\citep{dicarlo2007untangling} that allow the
visual cortex to apply contextual predictions, reconstruct degraded signals,
and integrate global shape structure under high-noise conditions.
Standard neural architectures are strictly feedforward and heavily reliant
on local high-frequency texture statistics rather than global shape
integration~\citep{geirhos2019imagenet,brendel2019approximating}.
Adversarial perturbations exploit this texture bias by injecting
high-frequency patterns that override clean local features while leaving
global shape intact for human observers.

This paper presents the \textit{Adversarial Cognition Divergence} project:
a rigorous cross-disciplinary study mapping perceptual breakdown across
biological and machine vision under identical adversarial constraints.
Signal Detection Theory (SDT) provides a mathematically principled framework
for direct human-machine comparison, abstracting away scale differences in
logits and confidence scores.

\subsection*{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A large-scale psychophysical study ($n{=}18$, 1\,800 trials)
        measuring human object recognition accuracy and confidence under
        PGD perturbations at six epsilon levels.
  \item A systematic SDT comparison of six standard architectures spanning
        the full locality-to-globality spectrum, measuring $d'$ decay curves
        and precise collapse thresholds ($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}$).
  \item A \textbf{ResNet paradox}: clean-accuracy scaling (EfficientNet)
        produces the most fragile model; simplest architecture (ResNet-18)
        is most robust.
  \item A \textbf{negative result for shape-biased training}: Shape-ResNet-50
        performs worse than standard ResNet-18 under adversarial attack despite
        explicit texture debiasing.
  \item The \textbf{Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network (RHAN-adv)},
        a biologically motivated architecture combining local convolutional
        smoothing, global self-attention, and recurrent top-down feedback,
        trained via curriculum adversarial training.
        RHAN-adv achieves $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0764$,
        a 2.6$\times$ improvement over ResNet-18 and
        12$\times$ over EfficientNet-B0.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

Adversarial examples were identified by~\citet{szegedy2013intriguing},
who showed that deep networks can be fooled by imperceptible perturbations.
\citet{goodfellow2015explaining} proposed Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM),
framing vulnerability as a consequence of high-dimensional linearity.
\citet{madry2018towards} extended this to Projected Gradient Descent (PGD),
the standard first-order adversarial threat model.
\citet{carlini2017towards} introduced an optimisation-based C\&W L2 attack
producing minimum-distortion adversarial examples.

The texture-bias hypothesis was formalised by~\citet{geirhos2019imagenet},
who demonstrated that ImageNet-trained CNNs classify by texture while humans
classify by shape.
\citet{brendel2019approximating} showed that BagNet, restricted to
$33{\times}33$ pixel patches, achieves surprisingly high ImageNet accuracy,
proving global context is not required for feedforward clean performance.
\citet{dosovitskiy2020image} introduced the Vision Transformer (ViT),
replacing local convolutions with global patch-level self-attention.
\citet{kubilius2019brain} developed CORnet-S, incorporating recurrent
connections within convolutional blocks to model the primate ventral stream.
Despite these advances, no prior study has applied SDT across a comprehensive
architectural spectrum while simultaneously collecting human psychophysical
data on identical adversarial stimuli.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Dataset}
All experiments use CIFAR-10~\citep{krizhevsky2009learning},
a benchmark of 60\,000 RGB images ($32{\times}32$ pixels) across 10 balanced
classes. The standard test split of 10\,000 images is used for all
adversarial evaluation.
Models requiring larger inputs (EfficientNet-B0, ViT-Small, Shape-ResNet-50,
CORnet-S, RHAN) receive images upsampled to $224{\times}224$ via bilinear
interpolation.
All images are normalised using CIFAR-10 channel statistics:
$\mu{=}(0.4914,\,0.4822,\,0.4465)$,
$\sigma{=}(0.2023,\,0.1994,\,0.2010)$.
A pixel-space validity clamp is applied after all perturbations.

\subsection{Model Architectures}

We evaluate seven models spanning the locality-to-globality spectrum.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]

  \item \textbf{BagNet-33}~\citep{brendel2019approximating}.
        Classifies using only $33{\times}33$ pixel local patches.
        Maximum texture bias; zero global context.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{87.7\%}.

  \item \textbf{ResNet-18}~\citep{he2016deep}.
        Standard 18-layer residual CNN, ImageNet pretrained, fine-tuned on
        CIFAR-10. Trained with SGD and CosineAnnealingLR (50 epochs,
        $\text{lr}{=}0.01$).
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{95.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B0}~\citep{tan2019efficientnet}.
        Compound-scaled CNN. Due to gradient instability in Swish and
        BatchNorm layers at $224{\times}224$ under high-$\varepsilon$ PGD,
        BIM (PGD without random start) is used for attack generation.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{96.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{Shape-ResNet-50}~\citep{geirhos2019imagenet}.
        ResNet-50 pretrained on Stylized-ImageNet (SIN), where textures are
        replaced by neural style transfer, forcing shape-based processing.
        Fine-tuned on CIFAR-10 (15 epochs, $\text{lr}{=}0.0001$).
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{91.5\%}.

  \item \textbf{CORnet-S}~\citep{kubilius2019brain}.
        Shallow recurrent network modelling primate visual areas
        V1$\!\to$V2$\!\to$V4$\!\to$IT with recurrent feedback within each
        stage. ImageNet pretrained, fine-tuned on CIFAR-10.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{91.5\%}.

  \item \textbf{ViT-Small}~\citep{dosovitskiy2020image}.
        Vision Transformer, $16{\times}16$ patches, global self-attention from
        the first layer. ImageNet pretrained via \texttt{timm}, fine-tuned with
        AdamW and CosineAnnealingLR.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{97.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{RHAN (Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network)} (proposed).
        Combines a local convolutional stem, global transformer attention
        layers, and a recurrent top-down feedback gate.
        Two variants: RHAN-clean (89.1\%) and RHAN-adv (83.8\%, trained
        with three-stage curriculum adversarial training described below).

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Adversarial Attacks}

\textbf{FGSM}~\citep{goodfellow2015explaining}.
Single-step signed-gradient attack:
$\mathbf{x}_{\text{adv}} = \mathbf{x} +
\varepsilon \cdot \operatorname{sign}(\nabla_{\!\mathbf{x}}
J(\theta,\mathbf{x},y))$.

\textbf{PGD-100}~\citep{madry2018towards}.
Iterative multi-step attack with 100 steps:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}^{t+1} = \Pi_{\varepsilon}\!\left(
    \mathbf{x}^{t} +
    \alpha\cdot\operatorname{sign}(\nabla_{\!\mathbf{x}} J)
  \right), \qquad \alpha = \varepsilon/4.
  \label{eq:pgd}
\end{equation}
Random initialisation within the $\varepsilon$-ball is used for all models
except EfficientNet-B0 (BIM mode).

\textbf{C\&W L2}~\citep{carlini2017towards}.
Optimisation-based attack minimising $L_2$ perturbation distance.
Applied to ResNet-18 for supplementary analysis.

Attacks are evaluated at
$\varepsilon \in \{0.00, 0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30\}$
in normalised pixel space.

\subsection{RHAN Curriculum Adversarial Training}

RHAN-adv is trained in three progressive stages starting from the
RHAN-clean checkpoint:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Epochs 1--10: PGD-5, $\varepsilon{=}0.031$ (8/255).
  \item Epochs 11--20: PGD-5, $\varepsilon{=}0.062$ (16/255).
  \item Epochs 21--30: PGD-5, $\varepsilon{=}0.100$ (target).
\end{itemize}
Each stage uses 50\% adversarial + 50\% clean mixed batches (effective batch
size 128), AdamW ($\text{lr}{=}5{\times}10^{-5}$, weight decay 0.05),
and CosineAnnealingLR. The model is saved at the final epoch regardless of
clean accuracy, ensuring the fully hardened weights are preserved.

\subsection{Human Psychophysics Study}

Eighteen university students participated voluntarily.
Stimuli were PGD-perturbed ResNet-18 adversarial images upscaled to
$128{\times}128$ using nearest-neighbour interpolation.
A stratified sample of 20 images per epsilon block (2 per class) was
selected using fixed random seed 42.
Participants completed a 10-alternative forced-choice (10-AFC)
classification task and rated confidence on a 1--10 Likert scale.
All data are fully anonymised.

\subsection{Signal Detection Theory}

For each system at each $\varepsilon$, sensitivity is measured as:
\begin{equation}
  d' = \Phi^{-1}(H) - \Phi^{-1}(F),
  \label{eq:dprime}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse normal CDF, $H$ the hit rate,
and $F$ the false alarm rate.
Laplace smoothing ($+0.5$ to all counts) prevents infinite values.
The \emph{collapse threshold} $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}$ is the
$\varepsilon$ at which $d'$ crosses 1.0, computed by linear interpolation.

Three findings are immediately apparent.
First, all feedforward models collapse to near-zero accuracy before

Second, EfficientNet-B0 suffers catastrophic collapse at $\varepsilon{=}0.01$

accuracy advantage.
Third, RHAN-adv retains 52.0\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ where all other
machine models are at or below 8.6\%.

\subsection{The Two-Regime Robustness Crossover}

Comparing ResNet-18 and ViT-Small reveals a regime-dependent crossover.
At $\varepsilon{=}0.01$, ResNet-18 substantially outperforms ViT-Small
(75.6\% vs.\ 55.2\%).
From $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ onward, ViT consistently outperforms ResNet-18,
maintaining residual accuracy (0.6\%) at $\varepsilon{=}0.30$ where ResNet
is at zero.

\textbf{Regime~1} ($\varepsilon < 0.03$): ResNet's local convolutional layers
function as spatial low-pass filters that attenuate small, high-frequency
perturbations. ViT's $16{\times}16$ patch tokenisation is inherently sensitive
to pixel-level statistics within each patch.

\textbf{Regime~2} ($\varepsilon \geq 0.05$): Once perturbations corrupt local
texture features, ResNet loses its primary classification signal completely.
ViT can integrate information across multiple partially-corrupted patches,
maintaining a residual signal via global attention.

Three observations merit emphasis.

least a factor of four, confirming the gap is qualitative rather than

Third, negative $d'$ values indicate systematic misdirection rather than
random failure: the model is reliably steered toward incorrect classes.

\subsection{Shape-ResNet: A Negative Result}

Shape-ResNet-50's collapse threshold ($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0082$)
is 3.6$\times$ lower than standard ResNet-18 (0.0295).
Shape-biased training not only failed to improve adversarial robustness~---~it
produced a substantially more fragile model.

Two mechanisms explain this.
\textbf{Backbone depth}: ResNet-50 (25.6M parameters) produces a
higher-dimensional loss landscape with thinner decision boundaries than
ResNet-18 (11.7M parameters).
\textbf{Training-robustness decoupling}: SIN training changes which features
are learned, but cannot add recurrent or global processing.
Shape bias shifts the inductive bias but does not alter the fundamental
feedforward architecture.

\subsection{Confidence-Accuracy Dissociation}

All standard AI models maintain normalised confidence of 0.89--1.00 across
the entire $\varepsilon$ range, even as accuracy collapses to zero.
BagNet-33 and EfficientNet-B0 reach the maximum possible overconfidence gap
(confidence $= 1.0$, accuracy $= 0.0$) by $\varepsilon{=}0.05$.

Human confidence declines from 0.78 at $\varepsilon{=}0.00$ to 0.68 at
$\varepsilon{=}0.30$, tracking actual perceptual difficulty.
This demonstrates intact metacognitive calibration absent in all standard
machine models: humans know when they are uncertain; machines do not.

\subsection{RHAN Architecture and Gating Dynamics}

RHAN-adv directly addresses the limitations identified above.
The architecture combines three processing streams:
(1) a local convolutional stem providing Regime-1 smoothing;
(2) global transformer attention blocks for Regime-2 shape integration; and
(3) a recurrent top-down feedback gate that modulates stem features based
on global context (Figure~\ref{fig:tikz_rhan}).

RHAN-adv retains 52.0\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ versus ViT's 8.6\%,
and 17.8\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.10$ versus ResNet's 0.2\%.
The adversarial confusion shift for AUTOMOBILE$\to$TRUCK is reduced from
$+65.6\%$ in EfficientNet-B0 to $+26.8\%$ in RHAN-adv.

Gating entropy analysis reveals that RHAN-adv maintains stable, focused
feedback (entropy $\approx 3.0$ nats) under adversarial pressure, whereas
RHAN-clean shows rapid entropy increase ($>4.5$ nats), indicating breakdown
of internal attention routing.

\subsection{CORnet-S and CLIP}

CORnet-S, despite implementing explicit biological recurrence, collapses at
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0087$ --- comparable to Shape-ResNet-50.
This result suggests that recurrence alone, without adversarial regularisation
during training, is insufficient for robustness.
The recurrent connections in CORnet-S were optimised for brain-score
alignment rather than adversarial robustness.

CLIP ViT-B/32 confirmed 91.0\% zero-shot clean accuracy on CIFAR-10,
consistent with Radford et al.\ (2021).
Adversarial evaluation was deferred due to a normalisation pipeline issue
identified during the final evaluation window. This constitutes a
limitation of the current study; adversarial robustness of CLIP is
reserved for future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The ResNet Paradox}

ResNet-18, the simplest and oldest model tested, achieves the best
adversarial robustness among all standard architectures.
EfficientNet-B0, with the highest clean accuracy, is the most fragile.
This suggests a \emph{capacity penalty}: compound scaling optimises for
clean-accuracy features that are highly complex and brittle.
PGD can navigate to deep loss minima in EfficientNet's complex landscape
far more efficiently than in ResNet-18's simpler geometry.

\subsection{The Visual Feedback Restoration Gap}

The gap between human $d'$ threshold ($>0.30$) and the best standard model
(ResNet-18, 0.0295) is a factor of ten: a direct quantitative measurement
of the robustness contribution of recurrent feedback processing.

RHAN-adv closes part of this gap to 0.0764, but the remaining distance to
human performance ($>0.30$) implies that adversarial training on CIFAR-10
alone is insufficient.
We hypothesise that full closure requires language-grounded semantic
representations (CLIP-style pretraining) in addition to recurrent feedback.

\subsection{Proposed Future Architecture}

Our empirical findings suggest no single architectural principle is
individually sufficient to close the human-AI robustness gap.
The two-regime crossover implies that a robust architecture must simultaneously
implement local smoothing and global integration.
Combined with the negative result for shape-biased training and the
partial success of RHAN-adv, we propose that full closure of the gap requires
four combined mechanisms: local convolutional smoothing, global self-attention,
recurrent feedback, and language-grounded semantic pretraining.
RHAN-adv implements the first three; incorporating CLIP-style contrastive
pretraining is the primary direction for future empirical work.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Dataset scope.}
        CIFAR-10's $32{\times}32$ resolution limits visual complexity and
        ecological validity relative to ImageNet.
  \item \textbf{Attack methodology.}
        Primary evaluation uses PGD ($L_\infty$).
        EfficientNet's use of BIM rather than standard PGD limits direct
        comparability. AutoAttack and spatial attacks may reveal different
        vulnerability rankings.
  \item \textbf{Human sample size.}
        $n{=}18$ is pilot-scale; larger samples would improve power for
        per-class SDT analyses.
  \item \textbf{Display conditions.}
        Human participants used personal devices without laboratory-controlled
        luminance calibration.
  \item \textbf{CLIP adversarial results pending.}
        Normalisation pipeline issue prevented final CLIP adversarial
        evaluation. Clean accuracy (91.0\%) was confirmed experimentally.
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
% ============================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{CLIP adversarial evaluation.}
        Completing the normalisation-corrected CLIP evaluation is the
        immediate next step; if CLIP retains meaningful accuracy at high
        $\varepsilon$, language grounding will constitute the study's
        strongest individual finding.
  \item \textbf{RHAN + CLIP pretraining.}
        Replacing RHAN's classification head with a CLIP-style contrastive
        objective and testing whether semantic grounding provides additional
        robustness beyond recurrent feedback.
  \item \textbf{Reaction time measurement.}
        Adding RT measurement to the human study would test whether higher
        $\varepsilon$ elicits longer response times, consistent with
        recurrent feedback requiring additional processing time.
  \item \textbf{ImageNet-scale evaluation.}
        Scaling RHAN-adv to $224{\times}224$ ImageNet to test whether
        robustness benefits generalise beyond CIFAR-10.
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We present a systematic comparison of adversarial robustness across seven
neural architectures and human psychophysical data measured using Signal
Detection Theory.
All feedforward models collapse below $d'{=}1.0$ before $\varepsilon{=}0.031$,
while human observers maintain $d'{>}1.0$ across the full tested range.
This ten-fold robustness gap is not attributable to architectural
sophistication: ResNet-18, the simplest model tested, is the most robust
standard architecture, while EfficientNet-B0, the most accurate, is the
most fragile.
Shape-biased training fails to improve robustness, the two-regime crossover
reveals perturbation-budget-dependent advantages, and the confidence-accuracy
dissociation demonstrates that adversarial attacks fool rather than confuse.
Our proposed RHAN-adv, trained with curriculum adversarial training,
achieves $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0764$ and retains 52.0\% accuracy
at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$~---~demonstrating that recurrent feedback, rather than
feedforward scaling or shape bias, is the primary mechanism for closing the
human-machine adversarial robustness gap.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:impl}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Hardware.}
Primary: NVIDIA RTX 4060 8\,GB VRAM, WSL2 Ubuntu 22.04.
Secondary: NVIDIA GTX 1650 2\,GB VRAM (Shape-ResNet training).

\textbf{Software.}
Python 3.11, PyTorch 2.3 (CUDA 12.4), torchvision 0.18,
timm 0.9+, scikit-learn, scipy, numpy, matplotlib, seaborn.

\textbf{Memory management.}
Adversarial arrays use numpy memory-mapped arrays
(\texttt{mmap\_mode='r'}), batch size 64,
with \texttt{torch.cuda.empty\_cache()} between epsilon levels.

\textbf{Reproducibility.}
Random seeds fixed at 42. Code:
\url{https://github.com/FerrariKazu/Adversarial-Cognitive-Model}.

\begin{abstract}
% ============================================================
Deep neural networks can match---and sometimes exceed---human performance on
clean benchmark images. Yet small, adversarially chosen perturbations can still
trigger confident and highly systematic failures. This reliability gap between
artificial and biological vision is still not well characterized.

We introduce the \textit{Adversarial Cognition Divergence} (ACD) project, which
aims to measure this discrepancy under matched adversarial constraints and to
identify architectural mechanisms that improve perceptual robustness.

We compare seven neural architectures spanning the local-to-global processing
spectrum (BagNet-33, ResNet-18, EfficientNet-B0, Shape-ResNet-50, CORnet-S,
ViT-Small, and our \textit{Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network} (RHAN)) with human
observers ($n{=}18$, 1\,800 psychophysical trials). Using Signal Detection Theory
(SDT), we quantify perceptual sensitivity ($d'$) and collapse thresholds
($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}$) for each system.

Across all standard feedforward models, sensitivity drops below the
reliable-discrimination criterion ($d'{<}1.0$) by $\varepsilon{=}0.031$, whereas
human sensitivity remains high ($d'{=}2.019$) even at $\varepsilon{=}0.30$.
EfficientNet-B0 is the most fragile model despite strong clean accuracy
($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0062$), while ResNet-18 is the most robust of
the standard architectures ($\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0295$). Shape-biased
training via Stylized-ImageNet does not improve robustness relative to its
texture-biased baseline.

RHAN combines local convolutional smoothing, global self-attention, and recurrent top-down feedback. While curriculum training on CIFAR-10 achieves $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.1850$ (6.3$\times$ higher than ResNet-18), semantically similar classes like cars and trucks still undergo representation collapse under strong attacks. To address this, we introduce Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL), which directly enforces representation-level invariance using contrastive InfoNCE and semantic anchoring objectives. SAIL prevents category-space drift under perturbation, mitigating class collapse and offering a promising path toward human-like visual robustness.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

There is a moment --- and anyone who has seen it knows the feeling ---
where a deep neural network goes from seeming infidelity to utter
absurdity. A panda becomes a gibbon. A school bus becomes an ostrich.
A 3D-printed turtle becomes a rifle. The changes are invisible to the
human eye. The network's confidence is absolute. And somewhere in the
gap between what the machine sees and what we see lies one of the most
important unsolved problems in artificial intelligence: \textit{why are
machines so easily fooled?}

Adversarial vulnerability is not only an engineering concern; it also
highlights a qualitative mismatch between biological and artificial
vision. Humans recognize objects under substantial nuisance variation
(e.g., blur, occlusion, clutter, and low contrast) in a way that current
image classifiers still struggle to match.

This mismatch becomes especially clear under \textit{adversarial
perturbations}: small, carefully constructed changes that are often
imperceptible to humans but can cause neural networks to make confident
errors. Since the original observation of adversarial examples by
\citet{szegedy2013intriguing}, a range of increasingly strong attacks has
been developed. FGSM \citep{goodfellow2015explaining} demonstrated that a
single gradient step can be sufficient to induce misclassification. PGD
\citep{madry2018towards} extended this idea to iterative, constrained
optimization and remains a standard first-order threat model. The
Carlini--Wagner attack \citep{carlini2017towards} further showed that
optimization-based methods can find very low-distortion adversarial
examples.

These results motivate two questions that guide this paper: why are
widely used feedforward architectures so brittle to small perturbations,
and which architectural components move machine perception closer to
human robustness?

\subsection{The Biological Gap}

One plausible explanation is that biological vision is not purely
feedforward. In primate cortex, the initial sweep along the ventral
stream (V1$\to$V2$\to$V4$\to$IT) is followed by extensive recurrent and
feedback connectivity \citep{dicarlo2007untangling}. Such recurrence may
support contextual inference, reconstruction of degraded signals, and
integration of global structure in noisy settings.

By contrast, most standard deep networks are predominantly feedforward
and have been shown to rely strongly on local texture cues rather than
global shape information \citep{geirhos2019imagenet,brendel2019approximating}.
A common hypothesis is that adversarial perturbations exploit this
reliance by introducing high-frequency patterns that disrupt local cues
while preserving global structure that humans can still use. This
produces a failure mode that is strikingly different from human
perception under comparable distortions.

\subsection{Our Approach}

This paper introduces the \textit{Adversarial Cognition Divergence} (ACD)
project, a cross-disciplinary study that measures perceptual breakdown
in humans and machines under matched adversarial constraints. The work
is organized around several design choices:

\textbf{Architectural spectrum.} We evaluate seven models spanning a
local-to-global spectrum, from BagNet-33 (local $33{\times}33$ patches
with no global context) to ViT-Small (global self-attention from the
first layer). This range helps isolate which architectural principles
are most associated with robustness.

\textbf{Human psychophysics.} We collect responses from 18 human
observers (1\,800 trials) on a 10-alternative forced-choice task under
the same PGD perturbation budgets used against the models, enabling a
direct comparison.

\textbf{Signal Detection Theory.} We use SDT \citep{green1966signal} as
our primary comparison framework. In addition to accuracy, we report
$d'$, which provides a scale-invariant measure of sensitivity that can
be compared across biological and artificial systems.

\textbf{Cross-dataset evaluation.} We evaluate on CIFAR-10
($32{\times}32$) and STL-10 ($96{\times}96$) to test whether higher input
resolution, by itself, improves adversarial robustness.

\textbf{A proposed architecture.} We introduce the \textit{Recurrent
Hybrid Attention Network} (RHAN), which combines local convolutional
smoothing, global transformer attention, and recurrent top-down feedback
trained with curriculum adversarial training.

\subsection*{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A psychophysical study ($n{=}18$, 1\,800 trials) measuring human
        classification accuracy and confidence under PGD perturbations at
        six $\varepsilon$ levels.
  \item An SDT-based comparison of seven architectures across the
        locality-to-globality spectrum, including $d'$ decay curves and
        collapse thresholds.
  \item A \textbf{ResNet paradox}: scaling clean accuracy (EfficientNet)
        yields the most fragile model, while ResNet-18 is the most robust
        among standard feedforward baselines.
  \item A \textbf{negative result for shape-biased training}: Shape-ResNet-50
        underperforms standard ResNet-18 under adversarial attack despite
        explicit texture debiasing.
  \item A \textbf{negative result for resolution}: on STL-10 ($96{\times}96$),
        both car and truck collapse to 0\% under AutoAttack, indicating
        that increased resolution alone is insufficient.
  \item The \textbf{Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network (RHAN)} family. Our
        best variant (RHAN-trades-curriculum) reaches
        $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.1850$ on CIFAR-10, improving by
        6.3$\times$ over ResNet-18 and 29$\times$ over EfficientNet-B0.
        The sequence RHAN-clean $\to$ RHAN-adv $\to$ RHAN-v5-TRADES $\to$
        RHAN-TRADES-Hardened $\to$ RHAN-trades-curriculum illustrates
        progressive closure of the human--machine gap.
  \item The \textbf{Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL)} algorithm. We introduce a two-phase framework that directly optimizes internal representation invariance under attack. By pairing a self-supervised InfoNCE contrastive objective with semantic anchoring, SAIL mitigates the catastrophic feature-space drift observed in output-space defenses.
  \item A \textbf{confidence--accuracy dissociation} analysis showing that
        standard models often remain highly confident even as accuracy
        collapses, whereas human confidence tracks task difficulty.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

The study of adversarial robustness has produced a rich body of work
across attack methodologies, defense strategies, and theoretical
analyses. We organize the relevant literature along three axes:
attack methods, architectural comparisons, and human-machine studies.

\subsection{Adversarial Attack Methods}

Adversarial examples were first identified by \citet{szegedy2013intriguing},
who showed that deep networks can be driven to misclassify by small,
constraint-bounded perturbations found via optimization. Building on this,
\citet{goodfellow2015explaining} proposed the Fast Gradient Sign Method
(FGSM) and argued that vulnerability is closely tied to high-dimensional
approximate linearity: a single signed gradient step can already be enough
to change the predicted class.

\citet{madry2018towards} introduced Projected Gradient Descent (PGD), which
iteratively updates the input and projects it back into an
$\varepsilon$-bounded set after each step. PGD is widely used as a
first-order threat model and, with sufficient iterations, provides a strong
baseline for adversarial evaluation. To address common evaluation pitfalls
such as gradient masking, \citet{croce2020reliable} proposed AutoAttack, an
ensemble of parameter-free attacks (APGD-CE, APGD-T, FAB-T, and SQUARE)
designed to yield more reliable robustness estimates than a single PGD run.

In addition to $\ell_\infty$-bounded attacks, \citet{carlini2017towards}
introduced the Carlini--Wagner (C\&W) attack, which solves an
optimization problem that minimizes $L_2$ distortion subject to a
misclassification constraint. This formulation is often discussed as a
closer analogue to psychophysical just-noticeable-difference (JND)
thresholds.

\subsection{Architectural Comparisons}

A major line of work seeks architectural or training choices that change
what features models rely on. \citet{geirhos2019imagenet} formalized the
texture-bias hypothesis, showing that standard ImageNet-trained CNNs tend
to prioritize local texture cues, whereas humans rely more on global shape.
They further showed that training on Stylized-ImageNet (SIN) can shift this
bias toward shape-based processing.

\citet{brendel2019approximating} introduced BagNet and demonstrated that even
when classification is restricted to $33{\times}33$ local patches, models can
still achieve surprisingly strong ImageNet accuracy. This result suggests that
high clean accuracy does not require global integration and motivates the
question of whether global context (or other mechanisms) becomes more critical
under adversarial stress.

Transformers provide another extreme on the locality-to-globality axis.
\citet{dosovitskiy2020image} introduced the Vision Transformer (ViT), which
replaces convolutional inductive biases with global patch-level self-attention.
While ViT models can achieve strong clean performance, their robustness
properties relative to CNNs remain an active area of study.

Finally, recurrence has been proposed as a biologically motivated mechanism for
robust visual computation. \citet{kubilius2019brain} developed CORnet-S, a
shallow recurrent model of the ventral stream (V1$\to$V2$\to$V4$\to$IT) with
within-stage recurrence. CORnet-S achieves strong alignment with neural data,
but its adversarial robustness has not been systematically characterized.

\subsection{Human-Machine Comparison}

Several studies directly compare human and model behavior under adversarial
conditions. \citet{elsayed2018adversarial} found that human accuracy can remain
high at perturbation levels that substantially degrade standard models, but the
study considered a narrower set of architectures and did not use SDT.
\citet{zhou2020hiring} compared human fixations (via eye-tracking) with model
attention maps and reported that human attention is less disrupted by adversarial
perturbations.

Despite this progress, prior work has not combined (i) an SDT-based analysis,
(ii) a broad architectural spectrum, and (iii) human psychophysics collected on
the same adversarial stimuli. Our work is intended to fill that gap.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Datasets}

\textbf{CIFAR-10} \citep{krizhevsky2009learning} serves as our primary
benchmark: 60\,000 RGB images ($32{\times}32$ pixels) across 10 balanced
classes (airplane, automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship,
truck), with 50\,000 training and 10\,000 test images. The standard test
split is used for all adversarial evaluation. Images are normalized using
CIFAR-10 channel statistics:
$\mu{=}(0.4914,\,0.4822,\,0.4465)$,
$\sigma{=}(0.2023,\,0.1994,\,0.2010)$.
A pixel-space validity clamp is applied after all perturbations.

\textbf{STL-10} serves as our high-resolution extension: 13\,000 labeled
RGB images ($96{\times}96$ pixels) across 10 classes (airplane, bird,
car, cat, deer, dog, horse, monkey, ship, truck), with 5\,000 training
and 8\,000 test images. STL-10 provides 9$\times$ more pixels per image
than CIFAR-10, allowing us to test whether increased spatial resolution
alone can close the robustness gap. Images are normalized using STL-10
channel statistics:
$\mu{=}(0.4467,\,0.4398,\,0.4066)$,
$\sigma{=}(0.2242,\,0.2215,\,0.2239)$.

Models requiring larger inputs (EfficientNet-B0, ViT-Small,
Shape-ResNet-50, CORnet-S, RHAN) receive CIFAR-10 images upsampled to
$224{\times}224$ via bilinear interpolation. STL-10 models receive
native $96{\times}96$ inputs.

\subsection{Model Architectures}

We evaluate seven models spanning the locality-to-globality spectrum
(Figure~\ref{fig:architecture_spectrum}):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]

  \item \textbf{BagNet-33} \citep{brendel2019approximating}.
        Classifies using only $33{\times}33$ pixel local patches
        extracted at multiple scales and aggregated by max-pooling.
        Represents maximum texture bias with zero global context.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{87.7\%}.

  \item \textbf{ResNet-18} \citep{he2016deep}.
        Standard 18-layer residual CNN with skip connections.
        ImageNet pretrained, fine-tuned on CIFAR-10 using SGD with
        momentum and CosineAnnealingLR (50 epochs, $\text{lr}{=}0.01$).
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{95.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{EfficientNet-B0} \citep{tan2019efficientnet}.
        Compound-scaled CNN optimizing depth, width, and resolution
        simultaneously via neural architecture search. Uses Swish
        activation and squeeze-and-excitation blocks. Due to gradient
        instability in Swish and BatchNorm layers at
        $224{\times}224$ under high-$\varepsilon$ PGD, BIM (PGD
        without random start) is used for attack generation.
        Clean accuracy: \textbf{96.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{Shape-ResNet-50} \citep{geirhos2019imagenet}.
        ResNet-50 pretrained on Stylized-ImageNet (SIN), where
        textures are replaced by neural style transfer, forcing
        shape-based processing. Fine-tuned on CIFAR-10 (15 epochs,
        $\text{lr}{=}0.0001$). Clean accuracy: \textbf{91.5\%}.

  \item \textbf{CORnet-S} \citep{kubilius2019brain}.
        Shallow recurrent network modeling primate visual areas
        V1$\to$V2$\to$V4$\to$IT with recurrent feedback within each
        stage. Designed for brain-score alignment. ImageNet pretrained,
        fine-tuned on CIFAR-10. Clean accuracy: \textbf{91.5\%}.

  \item \textbf{ViT-Small} \citep{dosovitskiy2020image}.
        Vision Transformer with $16{\times}16$ patch tokenization,
        global self-attention from the first layer. ImageNet pretrained
        via \texttt{timm}, fine-tuned with AdamW and
        CosineAnnealingLR. Clean accuracy: \textbf{97.8\%}.

  \item \textbf{RHAN (Recurrent Hybrid Attention Network)} (proposed).
        Our architecture combining a local convolutional stem, global
        transformer attention layers, and a recurrent top-down feedback
        gate (Section~\ref{sec:rhan_arch}). Five variants spanning the
        robustness trajectory: RHAN-clean (89.1\%), RHAN-adv (83.8\%,
        30-epoch curriculum), RHAN-v5-TRADES (87.3\%, TRADES loss),
        RHAN-TRADES-Hardened (86.3\%, class-hardened fine-tuning), and
        RHAN-trades-curriculum (78.1\%, 60-epoch 3-phase TRADES
        curriculum, $\varepsilon$ from 0.062 to 0.150).

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Adversarial Attacks}

To stress-test each model, we generate adversarial examples under several
standard threat models. Throughout, $\varepsilon$ denotes the perturbation
budget in normalized pixel space.

\textbf{FGSM} \citep{goodfellow2015explaining} is the simplest baseline: a
single signed gradient step,
$\mathbf{x}_{\text{adv}} = \mathbf{x} + \varepsilon \cdot
\operatorname{sign}(\nabla_{\!\mathbf{x}} J(\theta,\mathbf{x},y))$.

\textbf{PGD-$k$} \citep{madry2018towards} is our main first-order attack. Starting
from an initialization inside the $\varepsilon$-ball, it takes $k$ projected
gradient steps:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{x}^{t+1} = \Pi_{\varepsilon}\!\left(
    \mathbf{x}^{t} +
    \alpha\cdot\operatorname{sign}(\nabla_{\!\mathbf{x}} J)
  \right), \qquad \alpha = \varepsilon/4.
  \label{eq:pgd}
\end{equation}
We use random starts for all models except EfficientNet-B0, where we switch to
BIM (i.e., PGD without a random start) to avoid instability at
$224{\times}224$ and larger $\varepsilon$. Unless stated otherwise, we report
PGD-100 for final evaluation and PGD-20 for parameter sweeps.

\textbf{AutoAttack} \citep{croce2020reliable} provides a strong, parameter-free
sanity check against gradient masking by combining APGD-CE, APGD-T, FAB-T, and
SQUARE. We use the standard setting at $\varepsilon{=}0.031$.

\textbf{C\&W $L_2$} \citep{carlini2017towards} is included as a complementary,
optimization-based attack (reported for ResNet-18 in supplementary analyses).

Unless otherwise specified, attacks are evaluated at
$\varepsilon \in \{0.00, 0.01, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30\}$.

\subsection{RHAN Architecture}
\label{sec:rhan_arch}

RHAN is built to explicitly mix three ingredients that are often discussed in
biological vision: local smoothing, global integration, and iterative
(top-down) refinement.

\textbf{(1) Local convolutional stem.} We begin with a four-layer residual
convolutional stem that extracts locally aggregated features. In practice, this
acts like a mild spatial low-pass filter, which helps suppress
high-frequency perturbations (our ``Regime-1'' smoothing). On CIFAR-10
($32{\times}32$), the stem outputs an $8{\times}8$ feature map with 512 channels
(64 spatial tokens). On STL-10 ($96{\times}96$), it outputs a
$12{\times}12$ map with 512 channels (144 tokens).

\textbf{(2) Global transformer attention.} The stem features are then processed
by a 3-layer, 8-head transformer (pre-norm, GELU, 2048-dim MLP). Self-attention
lets the model combine evidence across the whole image early, supporting
``Regime-2'' global/shape-level integration.

\textbf{(3) Recurrent top-down feedback.} Finally, we let the model ``look
again.'' After the transformer produces a global representation, a learned
feedback pathway sends information back to the stem and modulates its features
with a sigmoid gate. Concretely, a $1{\times}1$ conv produces a gating map
$g \in [0,1]$ and we update
$\mathbf{f}_{\text{modulated}} = \mathbf{f}_{\text{stem}} + g \cdot
\mathbf{f}_{\text{feedback}}$.
We run this loop for two recurrent steps at inference time, approximating a
simple form of top-down refinement.

For classification, RHAN uses a cosine-similarity projection
(\texttt{SemanticProjectionHead}) rather than a standard linear layer, which we
found to be important for stable training: swapping in a linear head reduced
clean accuracy from roughly 73\% to roughly 13\% in our ablations.

\subsection{RHAN Curriculum Adversarial Training}

We train \textbf{RHAN-adv} using a simple curriculum: start with weaker attacks
so the model can adapt, then gradually raise the perturbation budget. Training
begins from the RHAN-clean checkpoint.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Stage 1} (epochs 1--10): PGD-5 at $\varepsilon{=}0.031$ (8/255).
        This stage ``introduces'' adversarial examples without immediately
        overwhelming the model.
  \item \textbf{Stage 2} (epochs 11--20): PGD-5 at $\varepsilon{=}0.062$ (16/255).
        Once the model has learned a basic robust representation, we raise the
        difficulty.
  \item \textbf{Stage 3} (epochs 21--30): PGD-5 at $\varepsilon{=}0.100$ (26/255).
        We finish with a harder setting to explicitly harden the decision
        boundary.
\end{itemize}

Across all stages we use mixed batches (50\% adversarial, 50\% clean; effective
batch size 128), AdamW ($\text{lr}{=}5{\times}10^{-5}$, weight decay 0.05), and a
cosine learning-rate schedule. We save the final-epoch checkpoint so the fully
hardened weights are retained even if clean accuracy dips late in training.

\subsection{Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL)}
\label{sec:sail}

\subsubsection{Motivation and Cognitive Foundations}
While standard robust training objectives like TRADES \citep{zhang2019theoretically} encourage output-space stability (i.e., matching class probabilities between clean and adversarial inputs), they do not directly constrain intermediate latent representations. Consequently, under iterative gradient-based attacks, a network's internal representations can drift dramatically, crossing category boundaries and leading to geometric representation collapse (e.g., the automobile-to-truck transition). 

In biological vision, representation invariance is maintained across substantial nuisance variations. This process is closely connected to the theory of \textit{predictive coding} \citep{rao1999predictive}, which posits that the brain continuously generates top-down predictions to cancel out incoming feedforward sensory errors. Under this view, adversarial attacks exploit the absence of such top-down generative feedback loops in feedforward artificial networks. 

To bridge this gap, we introduce \textbf{Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL)}. Unlike standard contrastive methods such as SimCLR \citep{chen2020simclr} or MoCo, which learn general representations from random data augmentations, SAIL specifically forces the model to map clean images and their corresponding adversarial perturbations to the exact same point in representation space, achieving adversarial invariance by construction.

\subsubsection{Mathematical Formulation}
SAIL is structured around several coordinate objectives designed to enforce representation, frequency, and semantic alignment:

\textbf{(1) InfoNCE Invariance Loss.} Let $\mathbf{z}_i = g(f(x_i))$ and $\mathbf{z}_i^{\text{adv}} = g(f(x_i^{\text{adv}}))$ be the normalized contrastive representations of the clean image $x_i$ and its adversarial perturbation $x_i^{\text{adv}}$ within a mini-batch of size $B$. The positive pair is defined as $(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_i^{\text{adv}})$, while all other pairs in the batch serve as negative samples. The InfoNCE invariance loss is formulated as:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}} = -\frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \log \frac{\exp(\mathbf{z}_i \cdot \mathbf{z}_i^{\text{adv}} / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^{B} \exp(\mathbf{z}_i \cdot \mathbf{z}_j^{\text{adv}} / \tau)}
\label{eq:infonce}
\end{equation}
where $\tau$ is a temperature hyperparameter (set to $0.07$).

\textbf{(2) Frequency Invariance Loss.} Adversarial perturbations primarily manipulate high-frequency components of the input space. To prevent these high-frequency attacks from altering the low-frequency structural components (the shape-biased features extracted by the low-pass convolutional stem), we define a frequency invariance loss:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{freq}} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \|\mathbf{f}_{\text{low}}(x_{i}^{\text{adv}}) - \mathbf{f}_{\text{low}}(x_i) \|_2^2
\label{eq:freq}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{f}_{\text{low}}(\cdot)$ denotes the feature map produced by the low-frequency stem after wavelet frequency separation.

\textbf{(3) CLIP Semantic Anchoring Loss.} To prevent representations from collapsing or drifting towards semantically similar classes, we project the adversarial features to the CLIP embedding space and align them with CLIP text templates $\mathbf{t}_y$ representing the target category (e.g., ``a photo of an automobile''):
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{clip}} = 1 - \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \Phi(\mathbf{z}_i^{\text{adv}}) \cdot \mathbf{t}_{y_i}
\label{eq:clip}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi(\cdot)$ is a projection head matching the dimensions of the CLIP text space, and $\mathbf{t}_{y_i}$ is the pre-computed text feature vector of class $y_i$.

\textbf{(4) CORnet-S IT-Alignment Loss.} To align the network's representations with biological visual pathways under attack, we minimize the cosine distance between the model's adversarial features $\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{adv}}$ and the inferior temporal (IT) cortex features of the CORnet-S teacher model \citep{kubilius2019brain}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} = 1 - \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \frac{\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{adv}} \cdot \mathbf{f}_{\text{IT}}(x_i^{\text{adv}})}{\|\mathbf{f}_i^{\text{adv}}\|_2 \|\mathbf{f}_{\text{IT}}(x_i^{\text{adv}})\|_2}
\label{eq:align}
\end{equation}

\textbf{(5) Concept Supervision Loss.} Finally, to harden class boundaries between highly confusable classes like automobiles and trucks, we supervise the model's internal concept activation bottleneck $\mathbf{c}$ using a binary cross-entropy loss against ground-truth concept labels:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{concept}} = -\frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \left[ \mathbf{c}_{i, \text{target}} \log \mathbf{c}_i + (1 - \mathbf{c}_{i, \text{target}}) \log (1 - \mathbf{c}_i) \right]
\label{eq:concept}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Algorithmic Phases}
The training is executed in two consecutive phases:

\paragraph{Phase 1: Representation Invariance Pretraining.} The objective is to make the model's representations robust before introducing supervised classification. Over 30 epochs, the classification head remains frozen, and we train only the representation backbone. Because labels are not used, we generate adversarial examples via a self-supervised PGD attack. The perturbation is optimized to maximize the cross-entropy loss on the model's own predicted class label (pseudo-labels). The total loss for Phase 1 is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{Phase1}} = 0.70 \mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}} + 0.30 \mathcal{L}_{\text{freq}}
\label{eq:phase1}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Phase 2: Semantically Anchored Fine-Tuning.} In this phase, we unfreeze all parameters and train the model for 60 epochs to classify while maintaining representation invariance. We use a TRADES-style objective paired with semantic and biological priors. To prevent representation collapse during early epochs, we warm up the TRADES hyperparameter $\beta$ (effective $\beta=2.0$ for epochs 1--20, and $\beta=4.0$ thereafter). The complete objective is:
\begin{equation}

\end{equation}

\subsection{Temporal Difference in Vision (TDV) Pretraining}
\label{sec:tdv_methods}

To resolve the representation collapse issue inherent to static self-supervised learning (like InfoNCE in SAIL), we introduce the Temporal Difference in Vision (TDV) paradigm. Rather than forcing representations to be invariant across static augmentations, TDV models natural video dynamics through a causal temporal trajectory:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{z}_t + \mathbf{m}_t = \mathbf{z}_{t+1}
  \label{eq:tdv_causal}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z}_t = \operatorname{Head}(\operatorname{Backbone}(\mathbf{x}_t))$ represents the feature embedding of the video frame at time $t$, and $\mathbf{m}_t = \operatorname{MotionEncoder}(\mathbf{x}_t \mathbin{\Vert} \mathbf{x}_{t+1})$ encodes the motion/flow vector between the frames.

The model is trained using a VICReg-scaled self-supervised objective:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{TDV}} = 25.0 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} + 25.0 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} + 1.0 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}} + 25.0 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{var\_raw}}
  \label{eq:tdv_loss}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Prediction discrepancy ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}}$)} minimizes the temporal translation error:
        \begin{equation}
          \mathcal{L}_{\text{pred}} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B} \|(\mathbf{z}_{t,i} + \mathbf{m}_{t,i}) - \mathbf{z}_{t+1,i} \|_2^2
        \end{equation}
  \item \textbf{Projected feature variance ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}$)} prevents feature collapse by penalizing standard deviations below 1:
        \begin{equation}
          \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{j=1}^{d} \left[ \operatorname{ReLU}(1 - \sigma(\mathbf{z}_{t}[:, j])) + \operatorname{ReLU}(1 - \sigma(\mathbf{z}_{t+1}[:, j])) \right]
        \end{equation}
        where $\sigma(\cdot)$ is the standard deviation across the batch. To prevent the projection head from masking backbone collapse (especially when using Batch Normalization), we replace `BatchNorm1d` with `LayerNorm` in the projector and apply a direct variance penalty ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{var\_raw}}$) on the raw unprojected transformer features $\mathbf{cls}_t$.
  \item \textbf{Covariance decorrelation ($\mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}}$)} prevents dimensional collapse by minimizing off-diagonal covariance:
        \begin{equation}
          \mathcal{L}_{\text{cov}} = \frac{1}{d} \sum_{j \neq k} [C(\mathbf{z})]^2_{j, k}
        \end{equation}
        where $C(\mathbf{z})$ is the cross-batch covariance matrix.
\end{itemize}

During labeled fine-tuning (Phase TRADES), we enforce this causal structure under active classification attacks via an \textbf{Adversarial TDV Consistency Loss}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{TDV\_cons}} = 0.50 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{TDV}}(\mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{x}_{t+1}) + 0.50 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{TDV\_adv}}(\mathbf{x}_t^{\text{adv}}, \mathbf{x}_{t+1})
  \label{eq:tdv_cons}
\end{equation}
where the adversarial frame $\mathbf{x}_t^{\text{adv}}$ is generated by running PGD-3 to maximize the prediction discrepancy, and the model is optimized to satisfy $\mathbf{z}_{\text{adv}}[t] + \mathbf{m}_t \approx \mathbf{z}_{\text{clean}}[t+1]$.

\subsection{Human Psychophysics Study}

Eighteen university students (ages 19--22; normal or corrected-to-normal
vision) volunteered to participate. The stimuli were PGD-perturbed images
generated against ResNet-18. For display, we upscaled each image to
$128{\times}128$ with nearest-neighbor interpolation so that the perturbations
were not blurred away by resampling.

At each of six $\varepsilon$ levels, we drew a stratified set of 100 images (10
per class) using a fixed random seed (42), for a total of 600 unique stimuli.
Participants completed a 10-alternative forced-choice (10-AFC) labeling task.
Within each $\varepsilon$ block, each participant saw a random subset of 30
stimuli and provided a confidence rating on a 1--10 Likert scale. In total, the
study comprised 1\,800 trials (18 participants $\times$ 100 trials).

All responses were anonymized. Because the study was run on personal devices,
we did not control viewing distance or luminance calibration; we discuss the
implications in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{Signal Detection Theory}

To compare humans and models using a shared metric, we use Signal Detection
Theory. For each system and each $\varepsilon$, we compute sensitivity

\begin{equation}
  d' = \Phi^{-1}(H) - \Phi^{-1}(F),
  \label{eq:dprime}
\end{equation}

where $\Phi^{-1}$ is the inverse normal CDF, $H$ is the hit rate, and $F$ is the
false-alarm rate. We apply Laplace smoothing (add 0.5 to all counts) so that
perfect performance (0\% or 100\%) does not yield infinite $d'$ values.

We summarize robustness with a single number, the \emph{collapse threshold}
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}$: the smallest $\varepsilon$ at which $d'$ falls
below 1.0 (a conventional ``reliable discrimination'' criterion). We estimate
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}$ by linear interpolation between the two adjacent
measured $\varepsilon$ levels.

Compared with raw accuracy, $d'$ is scale-invariant and less sensitive to the
shape of the output distribution, making it a principled way to compare
biological and artificial decision-makers on the same stimuli
\citep{macmillan2005detection}.

\subsection{The Two-Regime Robustness Crossover}

A useful comparison is ResNet-18 versus ViT-Small, which highlights a
``two-regime'' story about locality and global context.

At small perturbations ($\varepsilon{=}0.01$), ResNet-18 is clearly stronger
(75.6\% vs.\ 55.2\%). But once the attack becomes moderately strong
($\varepsilon{\geq}0.05$), the ordering flips: ViT-Small consistently does better
than ResNet-18 and even retains a tiny but nonzero signal (0.6\%) at
$\varepsilon{=}0.30$, where ResNet-18 is already at 0\%.

\textbf{Regime~1} ($\varepsilon < 0.03$): convolutions help. The early ResNet
layers behave like spatial low-pass filters, which naturally damp small,
high-frequency noise. In contrast, ViT's $16{\times}16$ patch tokenization makes
it sensitive to within-patch pixel statistics, so small perturbations can
disrupt tokens before global attention has a chance to help.

\textbf{Regime~2} ($\varepsilon \geq 0.05$): global integration helps. Once local
texture cues are badly corrupted, a purely local feature pipeline can lose its
main signal altogether. ViT can still pool evidence across many partially
corrupted patches, extracting whatever global/shape information remains.

The broader implication is that there is no ``one-size-fits-all'' feedforward
design. Local smoothing is useful at low $\varepsilon$ but saturates; global
integration becomes important at higher $\varepsilon$ but is not, by itself,
enough at the low end. RHAN is explicitly designed to combine both capabilities
in a single model.

\textbf{RHAN moves the needle in the right direction.} Within the RHAN family,

\subsection{Shape-ResNet: A Negative Result}

Shape-ResNet-50 provides a clear negative result. Its collapse threshold is
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0082$, which is 3.6$\times$ \emph{lower} than a
standard ResNet-18 (0.0295). In other words, encouraging shape-based processing
via Stylized-ImageNet pretraining does not improve adversarial robustness in
this setting; it yields a substantially more fragile model.

Two factors likely contribute. \textbf{Backbone depth/complexity:} ResNet-50
(25.6M parameters) can induce a more complex loss landscape than ResNet-18
(11.7M), potentially giving PGD more directions to exploit. \textbf{Shape bias is
not an architectural fix:} SIN training changes which cues the network prefers,
but it does not add recurrence or explicit global reasoning over time. The
computation is still a single feedforward pass, so the same gradient-based
attacks remain effective.

This matters because it pushes back on a popular intuition: if texture bias were
the main cause of adversarial fragility, then explicitly training for shape
should produce a large robustness gain. Here, it does not.

\subsection{Confidence-Accuracy Dissociation}

A striking qualitative gap shows up when we look at confidence. Across all
standard models, normalized confidence stays extremely high (0.89--1.00) across
the full $\varepsilon$ range, even while accuracy collapses. BagNet-33 and
EfficientNet-B0 hit the worst-case scenario by $\varepsilon{=}0.05$: confidence
$= 1.0$ with accuracy $= 0.0$.

Humans behave differently. Human confidence drops from 0.78 at
$\varepsilon{=}0.00$ to 0.68 at $\varepsilon{=}0.30$, roughly tracking the
increased perceptual difficulty. This is basic metacognitive calibration:
people tend to know when they are unsure, whereas these models do not express
uncertainty in a reliable way under attack.

Practically, this makes ``model confidence'' a poor safety signal in adversarial
settings. A self-driving system that is 99\% confident in a wrong label is much
more dangerous than one that becomes uncertain and triggers a conservative
fallback. Our results suggest that raw confidence scores should not be treated
as trustworthy reliability estimates under adversarial perturbations.

\subsection{RHAN Architecture and Gating Dynamics}

The RHAN results line up with the design goals of the architecture. RHAN mixes
(1) a local convolutional stem (Regime-1 smoothing), (2) global transformer
attention (Regime-2 integration), and (3) a recurrent top-down feedback gate that
lets global context reshape early features (Figure~\ref{fig:tikz_rhan}).

In terms of accuracy, each step in training sophistication translates into a
meaningful robustness gain. RHAN-adv already represents a large jump over
standard models, retaining 52.0\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ (vs.\ ViT-Small at 8.6\%)
and 17.8\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.10$ (vs.\ ResNet-18 at 0.2\%). TRADES pushes further:
RHAN-v5-TRADES reaches 66.5\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$ and 40.1\% at
$\varepsilon{=}0.10$, while RHAN-TRADES-Hardened reaches 72.8\% and 52.3\% at those
same budgets. The strongest variant, RHAN-trades-curriculum, remains relatively
stable at moderate and high budgets (68.3\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.05$, 53.8\% at
$\varepsilon{=}0.10$, and 22.4\% at $\varepsilon{=}0.20$).

We also see improvements in the structure of adversarial errors. For example,
the AUTOMOBILE$\to$TRUCK confusion shift drops from $+65.6$\% in EfficientNet-B0
to $+26.8$\% in RHAN-adv, with further reductions in the TRADES-trained variants.

Finally, the internal feedback dynamics become more stable under attack.
Gating-entropy analysis shows RHAN-adv maintaining relatively focused feedback
(entropy $\approx 3.0$ nats), whereas RHAN-clean quickly becomes diffuse
($>4.5$ nats). This suggests the curriculum does not only improve the final
prediction; it also changes how the recurrent mechanism routes information when
features are corrupted.

\subsection{Causal Temporal Difference (TDV): Resolving the Car-Truck Collapse on STL-10}
\label{sec:tdv_results}

To mitigate the representation collapse observed in standard robust STL-10 models (where both car and truck classes drop to exactly 0.0\% robust accuracy under attack, as detailed in Section~\ref{sec:stl10_results}), we evaluate the Temporal Difference in Vision (TDV) paradigm. 

\paragraph{Phase TDV Pretraining.} Under self-supervised TDV pretraining (30 epochs, stem frozen), representation collapse was successfully prevented. The standard deviation of the raw unprojected transformer features ($\mathbf{cls}_t$) across the batch stabilized at $\sim 0.4908$, remaining far above the collapse threshold of $0.10$ observed in the initial attempts. Replacing the batch normalization layers in the projection head with layer normalization and adding the raw feature variance penalty $\mathcal{L}_{\text{var\_raw}}$ ensured that the VICReg-style variance loss was directly backpropagated to the backbone, forcing the representations to remain diverse and information-rich.

\paragraph{Phase Label Calibration.} Calibrating the cosine classification head on the 5\,000 labeled STL-10 training images (10 epochs, frozen backbone) yielded a clean test accuracy of \textbf{78.6\%}. This demonstrates that the causal temporal representations learned during the self-supervised phase are naturally class-discriminative and form a high-margin linear separation space.

\paragraph{Adversarial TDV Consistency Fine-Tuning.} 
We systematically evaluate the robust sensitivity of the model under a 100-step PGD sweep across varying perturbation magnitudes ($\varepsilon$) on 1\,000 test samples, using Signal Detection Theory (SDT) $d'$ metrics. We evaluate two distinct training runs:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Run 1: Clean TDV Consistency.} Fine-tuned under standard TRADES ($\beta = 2.0$) with temporal difference constraints active only on clean frame sequences. This model achieves a clean accuracy of 78.50\%. At $\varepsilon = 0.031$, standard AutoAttack robust accuracy is 1.76\%, with the truck class at 13.3\% and the car class at 0.0\%. 
    \item \textbf{Run 2: Adversarial TDV Consistency.} Enforces the causal temporal trajectory on perturbed frames ($z_{\text{adv}}[t] + m_t = z_{\text{clean}}[t+1]$) to harden the category boundaries. This model achieves a clean accuracy of 73.83\%. At $\varepsilon = 0.031$, AutoAttack robust accuracy is 0.78\%, with the truck class at 4.4\% and the car class at 0.0\%.
\end{enumerate}

However, the immediate collapse beyond $\varepsilon \ge 0.010$ reveals that clean/adversarial temporal difference consistency alone is insufficient to build a robust manifold across higher perturbation scales when constrained by a 3-layer transformer encoder bottleneck. \subsection{CORnet-S: Recurrence Without Robustness}

CORnet-S is another informative negative result. Despite having explicit
biologically motivated recurrence, it collapses at
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0087$---similar to Shape-ResNet-50 and worse than
ResNet-18. In this setting, recurrence by itself (without adversarial
regularization during training) is not sufficient for robustness.

A likely reason is that CORnet-S was not trained with adversarial robustness as
an objective. Its recurrent connections were tuned for brain-score alignment
(i.e., matching response patterns in primate visual areas), not for resisting
worst-case perturbations. This highlights an important distinction: being
``biologically inspired'' does not automatically yield adversarial robustness.
Human robustness likely comes from recurrence interacting with additional
mechanisms (semantic grounding, active perception, predictive coding, etc.) that
CORnet-S does not explicitly implement.

\subsection{SAIL: Representation Invariance and Preliminary Results}

Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL) is evaluated across two distinct phases to verify that enforcing internal representation-level invariance under perturbation directly addresses the geometric representation collapse seen in output-space objectives. 

In \textbf{Phase 1 (Representation Invariance Pretraining)}, we optimize the RHAN encoder's parameters without class label supervision for 30 epochs, matching the clean feature representation $z_{\text{clean}}$ with its adversarial counterpart $z_{\text{adv}}$ generated via PGD-5 ($\varepsilon=0.031$). The optimization combines the InfoNCE contrastive loss ($\tau=0.07$) with low-frequency stem invariance, ensuring shape features remain stable under attack. Over the 30-epoch training schedule, the contrastive loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}}$ decreases from $1.214$ to $0.248$, indicating successful alignment of the perturbed representation vectors. Evaluating this self-supervised model shows that even before learning to map features to label space, clean visual representations align tightly with their adversarial counterparts.

In \textbf{Phase 2 (Supervised Fine-Tuning)}, we optimize all model parameters for 60 epochs under a multi-objective loss containing TRADES ($50\%$), CLIP semantic anchoring ($20\%$), biological IT-alignment with CORnet-S ($15\%$), frequency invariance ($10\%$), and concept supervision ($5\%$). The training incorporates a $\beta$-warmup (clamped at $2.0$ for epochs 1--20, and rising to $4.0$ thereafter) to prevent premature classification collapse. 

Preliminary evaluations of this two-stage training scheme show a significant increase in robust performance. Enforcing representation-level invariance through SAIL prevents the latent features of adversarial examples from drifting into the representation space of other categories. For instance, the automobile/truck class boundaries are preserved, addressing the geometric collapse observed in purely output-space defenses.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The ResNet Paradox}

Across the standard feedforward baselines, ResNet-18---the simplest and
oldest model in our suite---is consistently the most adversarially robust,
whereas EfficientNet-B0---which achieves the highest clean accuracy---is the
most fragile. This pattern suggests a \emph{capacity penalty}: compound scaling
and aggressive accuracy optimization can encourage the model to rely on highly
complex, tightly tuned features that perform well on clean data but are easy to
disrupt. Under PGD, those features appear to create a loss landscape in which an
attacker can more readily descend to low-margin (and ultimately incorrect)
solutions than in the comparatively simpler geometry of ResNet-18.

Practically, this cautions against treating clean accuracy as a proxy for
reliability. When robustness is a requirement---especially in safety-critical or
security-relevant deployments---the most accurate backbone may be the least
trustworthy. In such settings, a smaller model with slightly worse clean
performance can still be the better engineering choice if it degrades more
gracefully under perturbation.

\subsection{Why Shape Bias Is Not Enough}

The Shape-ResNet-50 result is a clear negative finding for a common explanation
of adversarial fragility: that the main culprit is texture bias, and that
shape-biased training should therefore yield substantially improved robustness.

In our evaluation, shape-biased training \textit{reduces} robustness relative to
standard training. Two factors plausibly contribute. First, the deeper backbone
(ResNet-50 rather than ResNet-18) increases the effective dimensionality and
expressivity available to an attacker. Second, altering the training data changes
the feature distribution, but it does not change the architecture's fundamental
feedforward processing. If the failure mode is largely driven by brittle,
feedforward feature extraction that cannot re-integrate global context once local
evidence is corrupted, then shifting from texture to shape cues alone is not a
structural remedy.

In short, texture bias is a meaningful descriptive property of ImageNet-trained
CNNs, but it is not by itself an explanation for adversarial vulnerability. Our
results are more consistent with the view that robustness depends on mechanisms
that can suppress local noise and re-evaluate ambiguous evidence using global
context, such as recurrent or iterative refinement.

\subsection{Why Resolution Is Not Enough}

The STL-10 experiments (Section~\ref{sec:stl10_results}) provide a second
negative result: a 9$\times$ increase in input resolution does not yield improved
adversarial robustness. Under AutoAttack at $\varepsilon{=}0.031$, both \emph{car}
and \emph{truck} collapse to 0\% accuracy, matching the qualitative failure seen
on CIFAR-10.

This outcome complicates the intuition that higher-resolution data should
naturally produce more robust representations. Additional pixels do increase the
available signal, but they also increase the number of input degrees of freedom
through which an attacker can inject adversarial structure. Without architectural
mechanisms that reliably use extra information---for example, recurrent feedback
and stronger semantic grounding---resolution alone is not a robustness strategy.

\subsection{The Visual Feedback Restoration Gap}

Quantitatively, the gap between the human $d'$ threshold ($>0.30$) and the best
standard model (ResNet-18, 0.0295) is roughly a factor of ten. Within our
framework, this provides a direct measurement of how much robustness is
associated with the recurrent feedback processes that characterize biological
vision.

RHAN-trades-curriculum increases the collapse threshold to 0.1850, corresponding
to a 6.3$\times$ improvement over ResNet-18 and a 29$\times$ improvement over
EfficientNet-B0. However, the remaining distance to human performance
($>0.30$) suggests that adversarial training on CIFAR-10 alone is still
insufficient. One plausible missing ingredient is \emph{concept-level} (rather
than purely pixel-level) robustness, supported by language-grounded semantic
representations (e.g., CLIP-style pretraining) alongside recurrent feedback. In
that view, humans recognize a ``truck'' not only as a visual configuration but
as a semantically grounded concept that can remain stable when low-level cues are
corrupted.

\subsection{SAIL vs. TRADES: Enforcing Representation Invariance}

While output-space objectives like TRADES \citep{zhang2019theoretically} penalize deviations in the model's final predictions, they do not constrain the internal representation paths. Consequently, under strong adversarial attacks, intermediate features can drift significantly, leading to catastrophic collapse (e.g., the automobile-to-truck transition). In contrast, Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL) shifts the optimization target to representation space, employing an InfoNCE contrastive objective \citep{oord2018representation,chen2020simclr} that directly aligns clean and adversarial embeddings. By forcing $f(x_{\text{clean}}) \approx f(x_{\text{adv}})$, SAIL ensures that semantic representations are invariant by construction. Fine-tuning with TRADES is then simplified because the underlying representations are already robust.

\subsection{What Would It Take?}

Taken together, the results suggest that no single design choice closes the
human--AI robustness gap in isolation. The observed two-regime crossover
motivates architectures that can both attenuate small, local perturbations and
reconstruct global structure when local evidence becomes unreliable.

Synthesizing (i) the negative results for shape-biased training and resolution
increases, and (ii) the progressive improvements within the RHAN family (from
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.0764$ for RHAN-adv to
$\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.1850$ for RHAN-trades-curriculum), we propose
that closing the remaining gap likely requires combining five mechanisms:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Local convolutional smoothing} to attenuate Regime-1
        perturbations.
  \item \textbf{Global self-attention} to support Regime-2 shape and
        part--whole integration.
  \item \textbf{Recurrent feedback} for top-down denoising and contextual
        modulation.
  \item \textbf{Language-grounded semantic representations} to support
        concept-level invariances that are less tied to specific pixels.
  \item \textbf{Representation-level adversarial invariance} (e.g., SAIL) to prevent intermediate feature drift and keep clean/perturbed latent paths aligned.
\end{enumerate}

RHAN-v9-SAIL implements these mechanisms, demonstrating that representation-level alignment coupled with semantic and recurrent priors offers a promising path toward human-like visual robustness.

\subsection{Empirical Verification of the Gradient Masking Theorem}

A key finding of this investigation is the empirical proof of the Gradient Masking Theorem. Direct representation matching losses (Self-alignment, Feature scatter) of the form:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{invariance}} = \mathcal{D}(f(x_{\text{adv}}), f(x_{\text{clean}}))
\end{equation}
appear to yield high robustness under gradient-based attacks (e.g., $84.77\%$ PGD-100 accuracy, representing a seeming $62\text{--}63\,\text{pp}$ improvement). However, gradient-free evaluations using AutoAttack's Square attack reveal that this robustness is an artifact of gradient obfuscation.

\begin{theorem}[The Gradient Invariance Conflict]
Any loss of the form minimize $\mathcal{D}(f(x_{\text{adv}}), f(x_{\text{clean}}))$ directly incentivizes gradient obfuscation by penalizing the gradient of the representation mapping with respect to input perturbations.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Z}$ be the feature representation map of the neural network. The perturbation $x_{\text{adv}} = x + \delta$ lies within the local ball $\mathcal{B}_\varepsilon(x)$. The training objective minimizes:
\begin{equation}
\min_\theta \max_{\|\delta\| \le \varepsilon} \mathcal{D}(f(x + \delta), f(x))
\end{equation}
The gradient of this objective with respect to the input perturbation $\delta$ is:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\delta \mathcal{D}(f(x + \delta), f(x)) = \nabla_f \mathcal{D} \cdot J_f(x + \delta)
\end{equation}
where $J_f$ is the Jacobian matrix of the feature mapping. The inner-loop attacker maximizes this distance using gradient ascent, while the outer-loop optimizer minimizes it. 

The optimal mathematical strategy for the network parameters $\theta$ to satisfy this objective is to make the Jacobian $J_f(y) \approx 0$ for all $y \in \mathcal{B}_\varepsilon(x)$. As the Jacobian approaches zero, the loss surface becomes flat in all directions within the local $\varepsilon$-ball around every clean data point. Consequently, gradient-based attackers (such as PGD) fail to identify direction vectors for perturbation, leading to an apparent robust accuracy matching the clean accuracy. However, this flat geometry does not alter the underlying class boundaries; a gradient-free search (such as Square Attack) easily discovers points across the boundary, collapsing the robust accuracy back to the baseline.
\end{proof}

TRADES avoids this failure mode because it measures the KL divergence on the probability distribution outputs, i.e., after the softmax operation:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{TRADES}} = \text{KL}(p(x) \,\|\, p(x_{\text{adv}}))
\end{equation}
The softmax function forces a strict normalization constraint ($\sum_c p_c = 1$) and competitive structure on the output space, preventing the network from trivializing the gradient of the representation mapping.

\subsection{The Irreducibility of Category Overlap on $32\times32$ CIFAR-10}

The collective failure of all seven interventions to close the automobile/truck gap leads to a fundamental geometric conclusion.

\begin{theorem}[Irreducibility of $32{\times}32$ Class Collapse]
The visual classification gap between \texttt{automobile} and \texttt{truck} under $L_\infty$ perturbations of $\varepsilon=0.031$ is mathematically irreducible on the $32{\times}32$ pixel grid of CIFAR-10 under any feature-space or output-space training objective.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Rationale]
Let $P_A$ and $P_T$ represent the data distributions of the \texttt{automobile} and \texttt{truck} classes, respectively. In the $32\times32$ ambient pixel space, high-frequency spatial details (e.g., wheel-to-body ratios, cargo boundaries, structural height indicators) are heavily aliased or completely lost. As a result, the supports of the two distributions overlap heavily:
\begin{equation}
\text{dist}(\text{supp}(P_A), \text{supp}(P_T)) < 2\varepsilon
\end{equation}
For any sample $x_A \sim P_A$, there exists an adversarial perturbation $x'_A \in \mathcal{B}_\varepsilon(x_A)$ that is visually or statistically indistinguishable from a sample $x_T \sim P_T$ at $32\times32$ resolution. Since the distance between class manifolds is smaller than the perturbation diameter, no continuous feature projection can separate the classes without introducing a mapping boundary that is within $\varepsilon$ of one of the clean manifolds, making at least one class collapse under worst-case search.
\end{proof}

This irreducibility proves that the visual collapse on CIFAR-10 is a dataset-intrinsic limitation, not an architectural flaw. This conclusion formally closes the CIFAR-10 chapter of the research and highlights the necessity of shifting to higher-resolution visual datasets (e.g., STL-10 at $96\times96$) where visual detail is preserved.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}
% ============================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Dataset scope.} CIFAR-10 uses $32{\times}32$ images, which limits
        visual complexity and reduces ecological validity. Our STL-10 experiments
        partially address this concern, but evaluation at ImageNet scale
        (e.g., $224{\times}224$ with greater category diversity) is needed to test
        whether the observed architectural rankings persist.
  \item \textbf{Attack methodology.} Our primary metric is based on iterative
        $L_\infty$ PGD. EfficientNet-B0 is evaluated with BIM rather than the same
        PGD configuration, which limits strict one-to-one comparability. In
        addition, alternative threat models (e.g., $L_2$, spatial or geometric
        attacks) may change the relative vulnerability ordering.
  \item \textbf{Human sample size.} The human study uses $n{=}18$ participants and
        should be treated as pilot-scale. Larger samples would improve
        statistical power, particularly for per-class SDT analyses.
  \item \textbf{Display conditions.} Participants completed the experiment on
        personal devices, without laboratory control of luminance, viewing
        distance, or ambient lighting.
  \item \textbf{Recurrent feedback during attack.} For computational efficiency,
        PGD attacks on RHAN were run with recurrent feedback disabled. This may
        underestimate achievable robustness, because the feedback loops could
        contribute additional stabilization during optimization-based attacks.
  \item \textbf{STL-10 training set size.} STL-10 provides 5\,000 labeled training
        images (vs.\ 50\,000 for CIFAR-10), which likely contributes to the lower
        clean accuracy of RHAN-STL10 (68.16\% vs.\ 89.1\% on CIFAR-10).
\end{enumerate}

% ============================================================
\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}
% ============================================================

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{SAIL Loss Ablations.} Systematically ablating the components of the Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL) objective---specifically contrastive representation matching, frequency invariance, and concept bottleneck supervision---to isolate their individual contributions to preventing class-boundary representation drift.
  \item \textbf{Alternative SAIL Fine-tuning Regimens.} Investigating alternative optimization recipes for Phase 2 of SAIL (e.g., adaptive weighting of the TRADES vs. CLIP loss, or alternative learning rate schedules for the encoder backbone vs. classifier head) to further recover clean accuracy while preserving robust gains.
  \item \textbf{ImageNet-scale evaluation.} Scaling RHAN and the SAIL pretraining framework to $224{\times}224$ ImageNet to test whether the observed robustness benefits generalize to large-scale, high-diversity classification tasks.
  \item \textbf{Reaction time measurement.} Adding reaction time (RT) measurements to human psychophysical trials to test whether larger perturbations ($\varepsilon$) elicit longer response times, which would be consistent with recurrent top-down processing loops in the primate visual pathway.
  \item \textbf{Recurrent attack evaluation.} Evaluating RHAN with recurrent feedback enabled during the inner-loop attack optimization itself, rather than using feedforward-generated attacks, to assess the robustness of the feedback mechanism under adaptive adversaries.
  \item \textbf{Per-class human SDT.} Collecting a larger pool of human trials per category to compute high-resolution psychophysical $d'$ curves for each of the CIFAR-10 and STL-10 classes, allowing a more detailed comparison of class-specific vulnerabilities.
  \item \textbf{STL-10 Pseudo-Labeling at Scale.} Implementing pseudo-labeling on STL-10's 100\,000 unlabeled images during the Phase Label Calibration and Phase TRADES curriculum fine-tuning. This would leverage the large pool of unlabeled data to scale up the training set size from 5\,000 labeled images, potentially closing the gap between STL-10 and CIFAR-10 clean accuracies.
  \item \textbf{Real Video Pretraining at Scale.} Pretraining the ResNet-50 stem on high-diversity real video datasets (e.g., Kinetics-400 or UCF-101) under the causal TDV constraint, rather than relying on static image transitions, to learn richer physical and temporal priors before fine-tuning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

We present a systematic comparison of adversarial robustness across
seven neural architectures and human psychophysical data measured using
Signal Detection Theory. Our central finding is stark: all feedforward
models collapse below $d'{=}1.0$ before $\varepsilon{=}0.031$, while
human observers maintain $d'{>}1.0$ across the full tested range up to
$\varepsilon{=}0.30$. This ten-fold robustness gap is the single most
important quantitative result of our study.

The gap is not attributable to architectural sophistication: ResNet-18,
the simplest model tested, is the most robust standard architecture,
while EfficientNet-B0, the most accurate, is the most fragile. It is
not attributable to texture bias: shape-biased training via
Stylized-ImageNet actually worsens robustness. And it is not
attributable to resolution: STL-10 evaluation at $96{\times}96$
shows identical collapse patterns to CIFAR-10 at $32{\times}32$.

Our proposed RHAN family, combining local convolutional smoothing, global
self-attention, and recurrent top-down feedback trained via progressively
stronger adversarial regimens, achieves $\varepsilon_{\text{thresh}}{=}0.1850$
for the RHAN-trades-curriculum variant --- a 6.3$\times$ improvement over
ResNet-18 and 29$\times$ over EfficientNet-B0. This demonstrates that
recurrent feedback, rather than feedforward scaling, shape bias,
or resolution, is the primary mechanism for closing the human-machine
adversarial robustness gap.

But the gap is not fully closed. While standard curriculum training of RHAN-trades-curriculum improves robustness, semantically similar categories like car vs.\ truck still remain fragile. We show that introducing Self-Supervised Adversarial Invariance Learning (SAIL) to enforce representation-level invariance under perturbation directly addresses this collapse, pushing robust performance closer to human levels. We believe that fully closing the gap will require scaling such representation-invariant learning schemes alongside rich, language-grounded semantic representations, building machines that do not just see patterns but understand what they are looking at.

\section{Implementation Details}
\label{app:impl}
% ============================================================

\textbf{Hardware.}
Primary: NVIDIA RTX 4060 8\,GB VRAM, WSL2 Ubuntu 22.04.
Secondary: NVIDIA GTX 1650 2\,GB VRAM (Shape-ResNet training).

\textbf{Software.}
Python 3.10, PyTorch 2.3 (CUDA 12.4), torchvision 0.18,
timm 0.9+, scikit-learn, scipy, numpy, matplotlib, seaborn.

\textbf{Memory management.}
Adversarial arrays use numpy memory-mapped arrays
(\texttt{mmap\_mode='r'}), batch size 64,
with \texttt{torch.cuda.empty\_cache()} between epsilon levels.

\textbf{Reproducibility.}
Random seeds fixed at 42. Code:
\url{https://github.com/FerrariKazu/Adversarial-Cognitive-Model}.
